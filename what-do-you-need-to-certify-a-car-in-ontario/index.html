What Do You Need To Certify A Car In Ontario

Written by Eveline Sep 02, 2023 · 4 min read

Are you planning on buying or selling a car in Ontario? The process can be overwhelming, especially when it comes to certifying the vehicle. Not to worry, we’ve got you covered. In this post, we will go over everything you need to know about certifying a car in Ontario.

When it comes to certifying a car in Ontario, there are a few pain points that you may encounter. For example, you may not know what documents you need to bring, what repairs need to be done, or how much it will cost. Additionally, the rules and regulations around certification can be confusing, and it’s important to make sure you have everything in order to avoid any issues down the line.

In order to certify a car in Ontario, you will need to bring the following documents to your local ServiceOntario centre: the vehicle permit (ownership), proof of insurance, and a Drive Clean emissions test certificate (if required). You will also need to make any necessary repairs to the vehicle to bring it up to safety standards. Once the repairs are complete, you can take the car to an inspection station, where a licensed mechanic will inspect the vehicle and provide a safety standards certificate if it passes.

To summarize, to certify a car in Ontario you will need to bring the vehicle permit, proof of insurance, and a Drive Clean emissions test certificate if required. You will also need to make any necessary repairs and have the car inspected by a licensed mechanic.

When I was selling my car in Ontario, I had no idea what was required for certification. I ended up doing some research online and found out that I needed to bring a few documents and have the car inspected. Cost of Certification:

The cost of certifying a car in Ontario varies depending on the repairs that need to be done and the inspection fee. Generally, you can expect to pay anywhere from $100 to $500 for certification.

Repairs:

When it comes to repairs, there are a few common issues that may need to be addressed in order to certify a car in Ontario. These include issues with the brakes, tires, lights, and exhaust system. It’s important to have these issues addressed as soon as possible to avoid any safety concerns on the road.

How to Find an Inspection Station:

In order to find an inspection station in Ontario, you can visit the Ministry of Transportation’s website or contact your local ServiceOntario centre. It’s a good idea to call ahead to make sure the station is open and to ask about any specific requirements they may have.

Personal Experience:

When I was certifying my car in Ontario, I had to bring it to a few different places for repairs and inspections. It was a bit of a hassle, but I was glad to know that my car was safe and roadworthy when I was finished.

Question and Answer:

Q: Do I need to get a safety standards certificate every year?

A: No, you only need to get a safety standards certificate when you buy or sell a car, or if you are bringing a car into Ontario from another province or country.

Q: How long does it take to get a safety standards certificate?

A: The length of time it takes to get a safety standards certificate can vary depending on the repairs that need to be done. Generally, it takes a few days to a week to complete the process.

Q: What happens if my car fails the inspection?

A: If your car fails the inspection, you will need to make the necessary repairs and have it inspected again. You may also need to pay an additional inspection fee.

Q: Can I sell a car without certifying it?

A: No, it is illegal to sell a car in Ontario without first certifying it.

Conclusion of What Do You Need to Certify a Car in Ontario:

Certifying a car in Ontario may seem daunting, but with the right information and preparation, it can be a smooth process. Remember to bring the necessary documents, make any necessary repairs, and have the car inspected by a licensed mechanic. By following these steps, you can ensure that your car is safe and roadworthy, and you can avoid any legal issues down the line.
