How Much Do Project Managers Make A Year

Written by Robby Jul 27, 2023 Â· 4 min read

Are you considering a career in project management? Or are you already a project manager who is curious about how much you can expect to earn? Either way, you've come to the right place. In this post, we'll explore the topic of project manager salaries and give you an idea of what you can expect to earn.

One of the biggest pain points for people considering a career in project management is uncertainty about the salary. It can be difficult to know what to expect, especially since salaries can vary widely based on factors like industry, location, and experience. Additionally, many people are unsure about what the typical career trajectory looks like for project managers, and how that impacts their earning potential.

So, how much do project managers make a year? The answer, as you might expect, is that it varies. According to data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the median annual wage for project managers in the United States was $74,200 as of May 2020. However, this figure can range from around $47,000 to over $140,000 depending on the factors mentioned above.

My Experience with Project Manager Salaries

As someone who has worked in project management for over a decade, I can tell you that the salary range for this field can be quite broad. When I started out as a project coordinator, I was making around $40,000 per year. However, as I gained more experience and took on more responsibility, my salary increased to around $90,000 per year. Of course, this is just one example, and your own experience may be different depending on your industry and location.

Factors That Affect Project Manager Salaries

So, what factors contribute to the wide range of salaries for project managers? Here are a few key considerations:

Industry

The industry you work in can have a big impact on your earning potential as a project manager. For example, project managers in the technology sector tend to earn higher salaries than those in the nonprofit sector.

Experience

As with most careers, experience is a major factor in determining your earning potential as a project manager. Those who are just starting out in the field can expect to earn less than those with many years of experience under their belts.

Education and Certifications

While not always required, having a formal education or relevant certifications can help boost your earning potential as a project manager. Many employers prefer candidates who have a bachelor's degree in a related field, and certifications like the Project Management Professional (PMP) can demonstrate your expertise and commitment to the field.

Location

Finally, where you live and work can play a role in your salary as a project manager. Salaries tend to be higher in large metropolitan areas with high costs of living, but keep in mind that this can also mean higher expenses.

Question and Answer

Q: What is the average salary for a project manager?

A: According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the median annual wage for project managers as of May 2020 was $74,200.

Q: Can project managers earn more than $100,000 per year?

A: Yes, many project managers earn six-figure salaries, especially those with many years of experience and/or advanced certifications.

Q: Do project manager salaries vary by location?

A: Yes, salaries can vary widely based on location. Generally, salaries are higher in large metropolitan areas with high costs of living.

Q: Is a bachelor's degree required to become a project manager?

A: A bachelor's degree is not always required, but many employers prefer candidates who have one, especially if it is in a related field like business or engineering.

Conclusion

In conclusion, project manager salaries can vary widely based on industry, location, experience, and education. While the median annual wage for project managers is around $74,200, it is possible to earn much more depending on your circumstances. If you're considering a career in project management, it's important to do your research and understand the earning potential in your particular field and location.
